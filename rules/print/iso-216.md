## The Rule
- Every A-series sheet has an aspect ratio of 1:√2 (1:1.41421356...). The long side is the short side multiplied by the square root of 2.
- A0 is defined so its area equals 1 square metre before rounding to whole millimetres: 841 x 1189 mm (actual area 0.999949 m²).
- Cutting or folding any An sheet in half across its long edge produces two A(n+1) sheets with the exact same 1:√2 ratio. The long side of A(n+1) equals the short side of An, which is what lets the sizes nest and scale predictably.
- Each size is numbered by how many times it has been halved from A0: A1 is half of A0, A2 is half of A1, and so on.
- ISO 216 also defines the B series for cases where an A-size is too small a jump. Bn's sides are the geometric mean of An and A(n-1) at the same position, still on the 1:√2 ratio.
- The C series, defined in the related ISO 269 for envelopes, sits between A and B. Cn's sides are the geometric mean of An and Bn, sized to hold an An sheet with a little clearance.

## Why
- A 1:√2 rectangle is the only proportion where bisecting it along the long side yields two rectangles similar to the original. With sides 1 and √2, halving the long side gives sides 1 and √2/2, the same 1:√2 ratio, rotated 90 degrees.
- Any other starting ratio loses this property after one halving. Every subsequent size would then need a different, non-proportional shape.
- The advantage of a √2 ratio was first described by Georg Christoph Lichtenberg in an October 1786 letter. Walter Porstmann developed it into a full system tied to the metric system, published as German standard DIN 476 in 1922. DIN 476 was adopted internationally as ISO 216 in 1975.
- B sits one geometric-mean step away from A (Bn = sqrt(An x A(n-1))). Its sizes fall roughly midway between two A-sizes.
- C sits one geometric-mean step from both A and B (Cn = sqrt(An x Bn)), much closer to An than B, close enough to sleeve it with a few millimetres of clearance. This explains the practical order: A used directly, C sleeves it (envelopes), B fills the larger gaps (posters, books).

## When It Breaks
- The United States and Canada do not use ISO 216. They use ANSI/North American sizes such as Letter (8.5 x 11 in) and Legal (8.5 x 14 in), which have no fixed aspect ratio relationship between sizes.
- Some print production uses custom trim sizes outside the A-series for cost, press-sheet imposition, or bleed reasons, even in ISO 216 countries.
- Envelope and some B/C series formats defined alongside ISO 216 (for folders, envelopes) do not always share the exact 1:√2 ratio of the A-series itself.
- Digital-only design (web, app UI) has no physical sheet to fold in half. There is no structural reason to constrain layouts to 1:√2; screens use their own aspect ratios.

## In Practice
- Scaling a print layout from A4 to A3 at 141.4% (100 x √2) keeps every proportion and grid relationship correct, since A3 is exactly A4 at that scale. The same layout works as a poster (A0/A1), brochure (A4), or flyer (A5/A6) without re-cropping.
- Envelope selection follows C directly from A: C4 (229 x 324 mm) holds an A4 sheet flat, C5 (162 x 229 mm) holds an A4 folded once or an A5 flat, C6 (114 x 162 mm) holds an A4 folded twice or an A6 flat.
- B-series sizes fill gaps where A alone jumps too far: B5 (176 x 250 mm) is a common hardcover book trim. B7 (88 x 125 mm), not B5, matches the ID-3 passport size. B1/B0 covers posters where A0 would be too small a step up.
