## The Rule
- Do not place the crop edge of a photograph directly at a joint: neck, shoulder, elbow, wrist, hip, knee, or ankle.
- Crop clearly above or clearly below a joint, not immediately adjacent to it, through the middle of a limb segment (upper arm, forearm, thigh, calf) wherever the frame allows. Practitioner heuristic: roughly 10% of the segment's length away from the nearest joint, no controlled study establishes this exact percentage.
- The rule applies per limb segment, so the safe zone scales with the subject's framing. A close crop of a hand has a much smaller safe margin in absolute pixels than a full-body shot.
- When the composition forces a crop near a joint, move the crop line to the nearest available midpoint rather than splitting the joint itself, even if that changes the intended framing.

## Why
- A crop line through a joint tends to read as an unnaturally truncated limb, since the cut lands exactly where the body would naturally bend or end. Plausible perceptual account, not an effect confirmed by a dedicated experiment.
- A crop through the middle of a limb segment is generally perceived as more intentional and visually comfortable, consistent with the Gestalt principle of closure (the viewer mentally completes a partially hidden shape), though no study tests closure specifically on cropped limbs.
- This is a documented convention in portrait and editorial photography instruction (e.g. Valenzuela, Picture Perfect Practice), not a result derived from a vision-science experiment on amputation perception.

## When It Breaks
- Extreme close-ups (a single eye, a hand holding an object) intentionally abandon full-body legibility. The joint-cropping rule does not apply once the frame no longer implies a whole figure.
- Deliberate fragmentation as a stylistic choice (fashion editorial, abstract figure studies) can crop at a joint on purpose for a disquieting or graphic effect. The rule describes the default that reads as "accidental amputation," not a hard limit on artistic intent.
- Group or crowd photography where individual limbs are partially occluded by other people does not trigger the same read, because occlusion by another body reads as depth, not amputation.

## In Practice
- In a CMS or page builder image cropper, add a visual guide overlay marking joint positions on the reference image so editors can drag the crop handle to a segment midpoint instead of a joint.
- For hero images and portrait thumbnails generated by automatic aspect-ratio cropping (object-fit: cover with a fixed focal point), manually set the focal point below the safe zone check, or supply a manual crop instead of relying on automatic center-cropping when the source photo includes visible limbs near the frame edge.

## Key Numbers
- **~10%** — Practitioner heuristic, not experimentally established: margin from joint per limb length
